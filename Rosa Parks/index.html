Rosa Parks

"Each person must live their life as a model for others." - Rosa Parks

1913 - 2005

Introduction

Rosa Parks, the "Mother of the Civil Rights Movement," epitomizes courage and resilience. Born on February 4, 1913, in Tuskegee, Alabama, her refusal to surrender her bus seat in December 1955 sparked the Montgomery Bus Boycott, dismantling racial segregation. Parks' words echo her commitment: "I wanted to be free so others would be also free." Her story is a testament to individual actions igniting transformative change. Today, we honor her legacy and draw inspiration for a world where equality and justice prevail.

Timeline

  • 1913: Rosa Louise McCauley Parks is born on February 4 in Tuskegee, Alabama.
  • 1932: Rosa marries Raymond Parks, a barber and active member of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP).
  • 1943: Rosa becomes the secretary of the Montgomery chapter of the NAACP, where she works on civil rights issues.
  • 1955: On December 1, Rosa Parks refuses to give up her seat to a white man on a Montgomery bus. Her arrest sparks the Montgomery Bus Boycott, a pivotal moment in the Civil Rights Movement.
  • 1956: The United States Supreme Court rules in Browder v. Gayle, declaring Alabama's bus segregation laws unconstitutional, marking a significant victory for the Civil Rights Movement.
  • 1957: Rosa and her husband move to Detroit after facing harassment in Alabama. She begins working for U.S. Representative John Conyers.
  • 1965: Rosa Parks becomes a staff assistant to Congressman Conyers, a position she holds until her retirement in 1988.
  • 1987: Rosa Parks co-founds the Rosa and Raymond Parks Institute for Self Development, aimed at educating youth about civil rights and helping them develop leadership skills.
  • 1996: President Bill Clinton awards Rosa Parks the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the highest honor given to civilians in the United States.
  • 1999: Rosa Parks receives the Congressional Gold Medal, becoming the first woman to be honored with this award.
  • 2005: Rosa Parks passes away on October 24 at the age of 92. She becomes the first woman and second African American to lie in honor at the U.S. Capitol Rotunda.

Achievements

  • Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-1956)
    Rosa Parks' courageous act sparked a 381-day boycott, led by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., resulting in the desegregation of public transportation.
  • Legal Victory (Browder v. Gayle - 1956)
    The U.S. Supreme Court declared Alabama's bus segregation laws unconstitutional, marking a significant legal triumph for the Civil Rights Movement.
  • Relocation and Continued Activism (1957)
    Faced with harassment, Rosa Parks and her husband moved to Detroit. She worked for U.S. Representative John Conyers and served as his staff assistant until her retirement in 1988.
  • Rosa and Raymond Parks Institute for Self Development (1987)
    Co-founding the institute aimed at educating youth about civil rights and fostering leadership skills.
  • Presidential Medal of Freedom (1996)
    President Bill Clinton awarded Rosa Parks the highest civilian honor in the U.S., recognizing her significant contributions to civil rights.
  • Congressional Gold Medal (1999)
    Rosa Parks received the Congressional Gold Medal, making her the first woman to be honored with this prestigious award.

Legacy

Rosa Parks' courage and activism sparked monumental strides in the Civil Rights Movement. Her iconic role in the Montgomery Bus Boycott desegregated public transportation, inspiring a nationwide push for civil rights. Beyond this, Parks co-founded the Rosa and Raymond Parks Institute for Self Development in 1987, educating youth about civil rights and fostering leadership. Recognized with the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1996 and the Congressional Gold Medal in 1999, her legacy as a symbol of resilience and a catalyst for positive change endures in the ongoing pursuit of justice.
